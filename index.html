Common Claim Adjustment Group Codes

Group codes clarify who is responsible for the unpaid amount of a claim.

CO

Contractual Obligation

The amount is a discount or adjustment based on the provider's contract with the payer. This amount cannot be billed to the patient.

PR

Patient Responsibility

The amount is the patient's financial responsibility, such as a deductible, copayment, or coinsurance. This amount should be billed to the patient.

OA

Other Adjustment

Used for non-CO/PR adjustments. This might include a service that was paid at the wrong rate or a claim that requires more information before it can be processed.

CR

Correction and Reversal

A prior claim was processed incorrectly and is being corrected. This indicates that the original payment is being reversed and a new payment (or denial) is being issued.

PI

Payer Initiated Reduction

The amount is a reduction in payment initiated by the payer for an administrative reason, such as a penalty for not meeting a certain performance threshold.

Medical Claims Forms: A Quick Overview

Understanding the difference between the forms and the codes is key to successful billing.

CMS-1500 Form

This form is used for professional claims—meaning services provided by physicians, physical therapists, or other non-institutional providers. Beyond the diagnosis and procedure codes, the form is broken down into three main sections:

  • Patient and Insured Information: This section captures all the patient's personal details, including their name, address, date of birth, and their insurance policy number.
  • Physician or Supplier Information: This section identifies the provider. It includes the provider's name, address, NPI (National Provider Identifier) number, and their tax identification number.
  • Service Information: This is the heart of the claim, where you detail each service. It includes the date, place of service code, the CPT code for the procedure, and a diagnosis pointer that links the service to the reason it was performed.

UB-04 Form

This form is for institutional claims and is used by facilities like hospitals, skilled nursing facilities, and ambulatory surgery centers. It's designed to capture a broader range of services. The key difference from the CMS-1500 is its use of Revenue Codes.

  • Patient and Facility Information: Similar to the CMS-1500, this section contains all patient and facility demographics.
  • Admission and Discharge: This section is unique to the UB-04 and includes details like the patient's admission date and time, discharge date, and the type of admission (e.g., inpatient, emergency).
  • Revenue Codes: This is the most crucial part. Instead of listing individual CPT codes, the UB-04 uses four-digit revenue codes to categorize all services and charges, from room and board to specific lab tests.

The Language of Denial: CARCs & RARCs

Ultimately, mastering claims isn't just about filling out the forms correctly. It's about understanding why a claim was denied and how to fix it. This is where Claim Adjustment Reason Codes (CARCs) and Remittance Advice Remark Codes (RARCs) come in. Think of these as the "language of denial." They are the most common codes you'll see on an Explanation of Benefits (EOB) and explain exactly why a payer reduced or denied a claim.
